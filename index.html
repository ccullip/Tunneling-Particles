Particles moving through walls? Is it magic? Is it sorcery? Wellll.... not quite. But electrons can tunnel, and they do it quite often, too.

In classical physics, particles can be contained within a potential energy well—or a “box” that has zero potential energy inside and infinite potential energy at the edges. The particle oscillates within the box in a wave-like manner. This can be described with the wave-particle duality theory, which posits that particles, such as electrons, exhibit both particle- and wave-like behavior. Scientists have observed experimentally that electrons do obey these classical laws in some scenarios. For example, in a 1-dimensional box with V = 0 inside the box and V = ∞ at each wall, classical physics is sufficient to explain the particle’s motion.

However, upon lowering the potential energy past a certain threshold, scientists observed particles traveling into the barrier, i.e. the classically forbidden region. To explain this phenomenon, physicists turned to quantum theory, which states that particles are allowed to tunnel through the barrier if V > E but less than infinity. When a particle reaches this barrier with E < V < ∞, it can either continue through the wall or be reflected and continue in the opposite direction. The probability of the particle being transmitted through the barrier is described by the following equation: `P = e^((-4api)/hsqrt(2m(V-E)`.

The probability of tunneling is dependent on the particle’s mass, m, the particle’s energy, E, the potential energy, V, and the barrier width, a. Less mass, higher kinetic energy, lower potential energy, and a thinner barrier would all increase the probability of transmission. As the potential energy and the barrier width increase toward infinity, the probability reduces to P = 0, which follows the classical assumption. Additionally, as the kinetic energy approaches the potential energy, P → 1, and when E >> V, the probability of tunneling is 1. For the following examples, we will simplify the equation to be either P = 1 (100% chance of tunneling) or P = 0 (0% chance of tunneling) in order to show the motion of the particle in each situation.

Upon entering the barrier, the particle will exponentially lose energy as it moves to the right. If the potential energy barrier is not infinitely wide, the particle can tunnel through the barrier and resume oscillation on the other side. The amplitude of the wave on the right side of the barrier will therefore depend on the width of the barrier and the initial kinetic energy of the particle. By increasing the particle’s energy and decreasing the barrier width, the majority of the original energy can be maintained.

Particle tunneling, especially electron tunneling, appears in many areas of science and has led to some incredibly important advancements in quantum computing, astrophysics, and molecular biology. For example, particle tunneling has led to the understanding of electron transfer within proteins—which is vital for cellular function in humans. To travel from one part of a protein to another, the electron will tunnel through pseudo barriers, similar to the theoretical particle in a box. By furthering our understanding of quantum theory we will discover more and more about the rules that define our world at a nanoscopic level.
