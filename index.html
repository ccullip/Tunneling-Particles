Tunneling Particles

Tunneling is an aspect of quantum mechanics that attempts to explain how particles can travel through potential energy barriers. Classical physics tells us that if a particle, such as a proton or electron, were contained in a "box" with walls of potential energy higher than the particle's kinetic energy, the particle would not be able to leave the box. In a 1-dimensional box with V = 0 inside the box and V = ∞ at each wall, the classical movement appears to be true.

But what if the potential energy is still higher that the kinetic energy but not infinity? Well... This leads to tunneling! Quantum theory states that particles are able to tunnel through the walls if V>E, with the kinetic energy decaying as it tunnels. Tunneling is not guaranteed, however. When a particle reaches a boundary with E < V < ∞, it can either continue through the wall or be reflected and change direction. This can be described by the tunneling probability: `P = e^((-4api)/hsqrt(2m(V-E)`.

The probability of tunneling will increase as mass, m, decreases, potential energy, V, decreases, and the barrier width, a, decreases. In reality, the particle, which also has wave-like qualities, will mostly be reflected, although a certain portion will continue through the barrier, depending on the probability from the above equation.
